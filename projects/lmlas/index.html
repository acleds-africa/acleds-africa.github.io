Flagship Study Active

Longitudinal Mathematics Learning Analytics Study

LMLAS · 2026 — ongoing

A multi-year tracking study examining how mathematics confidence, identity, and achievement co-evolve across primary and secondary schooling — using learning analytics methods to surface patterns invisible to cross-sectional research.

Design Longitudinal, mixed methods
Duration Multi-year
Status Data collection ongoing
PI Prosper Ablordeppey

Background

Why This Study?

Mathematics performance in African school systems has been a persistent policy concern. But most of the research on mathematics learning — even research conducted in Africa — is cross-sectional: it takes a snapshot at one moment in time, compares groups, and draws conclusions. What it cannot do is track how individual learners change over time.

The LMLAS was designed to address this gap directly. By following cohorts of learners over multiple years, we can observe not just where students end up, but how they get there — and what factors along the way accelerate, sustain, or derail mathematics learning.

The study is grounded in the insight that mathematics confidence and achievement are intertwined but distinct. Students can have high confidence and low achievement, or vice versa. The relationship between them shifts over time in patterned ways that have significant implications for teaching and curriculum design.

Research Questions

What We Are Investigating

  1. RQ1
    Confidence–Achievement Trajectories

    How do mathematics confidence and measurable achievement co-evolve across the primary school years, and in what ways do they diverge?

  2. RQ2
    Predictors of Positive Trajectories

    What learner, teacher, classroom, and school-level factors predict positive mathematics learning trajectories over time?

  3. RQ3
    Mathematical Identity

    How does mathematical identity — the degree to which students see themselves as "maths people" — interact with achievement outcomes over time?

  4. RQ4
    Analytics Advantage

    What do learning analytics approaches reveal about mathematics learning that traditional assessment data alone cannot capture?

Approach

Methodology

The LMLAS uses a mixed-methods longitudinal design, combining:

Standardised Assessments

Validated mathematics assessments administered annually to track achievement over time.

Learner Surveys

Annual surveys measuring confidence, identity, engagement, and attitudes toward mathematics.

Teacher Observations

Structured classroom observations to capture pedagogical practice and learning environment quality.

Administrative Data

School-level and system-level data including attendance, progression, and national assessment results.

Qualitative Interviews

In-depth interviews with selected learners and teachers to surface meanings and experiences.

Learning Analytics

Computational analysis of longitudinal datasets to identify trajectory patterns and predictive factors.

Implications

Why It Matters

The LMLAS is designed from the outset to produce findings that can reach policy and practice — not just academic literature. Key implications we anticipate include:

  • Evidence to inform early identification of learners at risk of mathematics disengagement
  • Insights for teacher professional development focused on confidence-building
  • Recommendations for curriculum sequencing based on longitudinal trajectory data
  • A replicable methodological model for longitudinal learning research in African school contexts
