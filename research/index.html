Research

Our work spans three intersecting themes — each grounded in African educational contexts and oriented toward generating findings that matter.

01

Theme 01

Learning Analytics

Learning analytics concerns the measurement, collection, analysis, and reporting of data about learners — with the purpose of understanding and optimising learning and the environments in which it occurs. Our work in this area develops and applies analytical approaches suited to African school contexts, where data infrastructure and availability differ substantially from high-income settings.

We are particularly interested in how learning analytics can surface patterns in engagement, achievement, and learning trajectory that are invisible to teachers and school administrators working with traditional assessment data alone.

Key Questions

  • How can learning analytics be implemented ethically and effectively in under-resourced school settings?
  • What data sources are available and appropriate in African contexts?
  • How do learning trajectories differ across school types, geographies, and learner populations?
02

Theme 02

Educational Data Science

Educational data science applies computational and statistical methods to education data at scale — to surface patterns, test hypotheses, and generate models that can inform teaching and policy. Our work in this theme focuses on developing methodological approaches appropriate to the data environments typical of African education systems.

This includes work on data cleaning and curation, missing data handling in longitudinal studies, predictive modelling of achievement, and the responsible use of algorithmic tools in education contexts.

Key Questions

  • What statistical and machine learning approaches are valid for African educational datasets?
  • How can education data be collected and managed in ways that protect student privacy?
  • What can administrative data reveal about systemic patterns in learning outcomes?
03

Theme 03

Mathematics Education

Mathematics education is a central concern for African educational systems, where mathematics outcomes at the primary and secondary level have significant downstream consequences for access to higher education, STEM pathways, and economic participation. Our work investigates how mathematics confidence, identity, and achievement develop over time — and what factors predict trajectories of success or disengagement.

This theme connects directly to our flagship study, the Longitudinal Mathematics Learning Analytics Study (LMLAS), which tracks cohorts of learners over multiple years using both quantitative and qualitative data.

Key Questions

  • How does mathematics confidence develop (or erode) across primary schooling?
  • What is the relationship between mathematical identity and measurable achievement?
  • Which pedagogical and environmental factors predict positive mathematics trajectories?
